Morning of the Hanging Shoot




"Morning of the Hanging" was produced by a workshop funded by The
Academy of Motion Pictures Arts and Sciences, under the direction of
Mark Headley. Michael Fleming was a workshop participant with writing
& directing responsibilities. Other participants took on the
cinematography, editing, music, casting, and acting roles.

Headleyand Fleming make a movie together every 50 years, so they are
about due for a new production!




The eight minute western was filmed on Saturday, April 1, 1978 at Old
Tucson movie set and amusement park with a 16mm spring wound Bolex. We
had costumes from Old Tucson wardrobe that we got for the cleaning
fees, and we had free run of all the exterior sets. We had to pay $20
for the horse.





"The New Maverick" TV movie was using the courthouse set at Old
Tucson. Maverick was my favorite TV show when I was 10 years old in
1957. From the theme song: "Ridin' the trails to who knows where,
luck is his companion, gambling is his game" Sounds good to me!

The crew were behind the security lines for the production, so we had
full access to the set, and all that activity. I didn't visit, but it
was nice to be around the arc lights and all the camera sand equipment
for a Hollywood production.




One of the crew took the opportunity to ask James Garner if he would
come for a cameo in our movie.

During his lunch break, he came over to our set wearing the
distinctive Maverick costume, with a striped vest and his cowboy hat
with a distinctive brim. I explained that he was to refuse the
Sheriff's request, and walk down the wooden sidewalk. We had one
rehearsal. He looked at me, "Was that OK?'" I said it was.

The take with the spring wound camera motor running was even better,
with exaggerated gestures and facial expressions that suited the bit
perfectly.

Reportedly, he received $1m for a one minute tv commercial pitching
Kodak, so his bit was probably worth a couple hundred thousand 1978
dollars.


The crowd visiting Old Tucson were thrilled to see a real Hollywood
movie star. I remember the clicks of the shutters on the instamatic
cameras of the crowd. Our crew were in a lot of vacation photographs!

The narration and music recording was done in a sound studio in the
same session. Mark got together a few musicians who wrote original
music and also improvised as the film was shown in the sound studio.

A cowboy poet narrated the story. He added an authentic voice to the
story. He also improvised the best lines in the film.

Many talents go into film making, and the cast and crew exceeded
expectations in all areas.





Lee Garmes was the advisor on the film. He worked with the
cinematographer on the crowd scene, placing props and extras so as to
make the crowd seem bigger than it really was. He was generous in
sharing his knowledge and experience over a decades long career as
cinematographer on top Hollywood movies, including “Gone with the Wind”

and Shanghai Express with Marlene Dietrich.


Some other stories:

One of the workshop participants told me this:

Someone from the New Maverick production told James Garner that the
Old Tucson amusement park was using his name in their radio ads.

Garner looked shocked. "My name! They're using my name! I've had
enough. I'm outa here!" The crew was aghast. One of the stars of the
movie had just walked out endangering the schedule and every thing
else.

A beat later, Garner stuck his head in the door of the courtroom.

"April fools!"

The scene when the stranger loses his hat was unintentional. We
brought the horse back and set up the scene again. This time the horse
wouldn't gallop, and no amount of bouncing up and down in the saddle
would persuade the horse to hurry anywhere soon. It was sort of
comical.

This shot was getting expensive in terms of time on the set and film
stock. The horse was not galloping any more that day, so we had to
select the blown off hat take for the movie. It was sort of painful
since hats are not optional for a horseman on the Arizona desert

The workshop films were presented at Gallagher theater on the
University of Arizona campus. Lee Garmes was in attendance and praised
the crew's performance under great time pressures.

In 1980, KUAT-TV, the PBS affiliate in Tucson, put on a program about
film makers of Tucson and chose Morning of the Hanging for one of the
short films. I was interviewed as part of the show.

The next public showing was at the screening room in downtown Tucson,
with Mike and Mark in attendance.
